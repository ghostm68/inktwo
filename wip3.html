Lübeck, 1922

The city sleeps beneath its seven spires, each church tower a finger pointing toward heaven or perhaps away from what lies below in the narrow gänge—those medieval passages threading between merchant houses where footsteps echo differently than they should.

In the salt warehouses along the Trave, workers move cargo that traveled routes older than maps. The Hanseatic League is long dead, but its ghosts negotiate in the shadows of brick Gothic arches, their ledgers still tallying debts that compound across centuries.

This is the city Murnau saw when he came looking for death. Not the death in front of cameras, but the slower kind—the rot that starts in prosperity and spreads through the foundations. Every grand façade here leans slightly, imperceptibly, toward the water.
